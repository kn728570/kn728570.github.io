Inclusive Practices

Being an inclusive educator is a core part of my pedagogical beliefs. I believe that our classrooms need to be fun place for our students to learn. However, this requires us to take special measures to ensure that the classroom is fun for all of the students. This is what inclusive education means to me; doing whatever is needed to be done to ensure my students are having fun. This can mean adaptations for students who have issues, special consideration at including culturally relevant material in my lessons, broadening my assessment methods so that students can demonstrate their learning by choosing a method that suits them best, and much more. My thoughts on this matter are further explored in my writing on the BIPOC experience.

Being an inclusive educator often times doesn't require a lot of effort. Often, it can just mean being cognizant of students and their differences. For example, during my first practicum at J.L. Ilsley High School, I had a few Syrian refugee students who were still learning English. As a parting gift to all the students, I had copies of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms made for the Students. I had special copies of the Charter printed in Arabic for my Syrian students, while also providing them with the English copies that everyone else received. The students were extremely grateful that I had taken the time to provide them with copies in their native language. All this took was downloading an additional PDF of the Charter of the Government of Canada's website, and attaching that PDF with the English copy. Being Inclusive can simply mean being proactive and mindful, something I pride myself in.
